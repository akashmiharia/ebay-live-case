Seller and Host Automation

A point of view on eBay Live. What live commerce actually is, why it has stayed a gated beta for four years, the one problem I would solve first for the seller and host, and a working prototype of the agent that solves it.

Akash Miharia · Product Thinking & Prototype · July 6, 2026
What is live commerce

Regular commerce serves demand.
Live commerce manufactures it.

Traditional commerce · serves intent
1You already have an intent, a need or a want.
2You know where to go to satisfy it.
3You search, the platform serves it, the transaction closes.
Efficient. And completely without feeling.
Live commerce · manufactures intent
1You show up with no intent. You are there to watch, not to buy.
2Community forms the want. Think of the barbecue where friends talk up a grill until you want one, or that moment in Sephora when a real shopper says "this changed my skin" and you trust her over the clerk. That exact aha, a want forming socially from people who are not selling to you, is the feeling a live show creates on purpose.
3The auction makes it a moment. The countdown, the bidding, the win. The live format turns a purchase into a memory you were part of, not a transaction you completed.
Here intent is the output, not the input. That is what Amazon and Walmart are not built to manufacture.

Why only eBay can start it
Only eBay has all three at once: existing sellers, verified authenticity, and dormant high-value supply already listed. No competitor can convert that to a live show in one tap, and because the streams are yield-positive, eBay spins the flywheel profitably.

The two picks are not separate decisions. Removing setup toil for the dormant seller is the single push that gets the wheel turning. Solve one thing well, and the rest of the barriers start solving themselves.

The focus · what we build to spin it, and what we won't

To start the flywheel, here's what we build, what we match, and what we won't chase.

Below · trade it away

  • Out-communitying Whatnot's culture (their moat, fake on eBay)
  • Breadth of cheap, impulse goods
  • Being the lowest-fee option

Table stakes · must match

  • Go live from a phone, fast payout, discovery
  • Auction mechanics that don't glitch
  • AI listing and reply drafts (eBay already ships these)

Differentiate · where we win

  • The human keeps the room, the agent takes the ops
  • Trusted supply from existing data in one tap
  • Trust in the live moment: in-stream comps, Authenticity Guarantee, video receipt

Every piece of the prototype is one idea: the agent takes an operation, the human keeps the connection. I prioritized which operations first by frequency × pain × trust-risk: P0 the one-tap build and off-camera shipping and no-pays, then trust-in-the-moment (authenticity, the size-fix), then loyalty offers and safety.

Challenges to work through · where we invest, where I'd want buy-in

The challenges I'm anticipating, and the investment each one needs.

The layerA knowledge graph over eBay's catalog, comps, authentication, and buyer history, plus an event layer for the live moment. Converting static catalog into live-ready shows is the core capability, and eBay is uniquely positioned to build it because it owns the data.
Cold startSlower and richer. It runs before the show, so spend the compute and get the pricing and lots right.
In-showLow-latency. It must keep pace with a live room, so lean on the pre-built layer, not heavy real-time reasoning.
Human gateAnything irreversible, an offer, a price drop, a refund, the agent proposes and the human confirms. Bias to speed where cheap and reversible, to control where a wrong action is costly.

The real-time context layer is the foundation the whole thing sits on, and the first investment I would make. Reliability of the stream is the floor, and this is where I would want leadership's read early.

eBay Live, the one-tap host

The seller and host experience: how an agent turns any eBay seller into a confident, trusted live host, and how that graduates Live out of a four-year beta.

§ Executive summary

The bet. Activate eBay's dormant, high-value sellers with a one-tap host: an agent that clears the operations so the seller just runs the room. I would prioritize this over a training agent or the buyer side.

Why Live is stuck. Going live today means one person runs a creative show and a full operation at the same time. It is so much work that only a gated few can host, so Live has stayed invite-only since 2022.

Why eBay wins. Under every seller sits thirty years of catalog, sold-comps, and authentication that no competitor can copy, and it is exactly what an agent needs to build a trusted show in one tap. Buyers come to eBay to shop, so the trust is real.

eBay Live is the only place to sell high-value goods live to buyers who came to shop and can trust what they are buying, with eBay carrying the trust and the operations so the host can just run the room.

1 What live commerce is

Regular commerce serves an intent you already have. Live commerce creates the intent on the spot, socially. Five ways to feel the difference:

  • The barbecue. A friend shows off a new grill, the group says "you got that for that price?", and a want forms through the group, not through need. Live is the barbecue, not the charger.
  • The Sephora turn. A stranger says "I tried that, it's great for your skin," and you suddenly want it. In a store that is luck. On live it happens on purpose, to a whole room, over and over.
  • The auction thrill. Almost winning, the countdown, the fear of missing the drop. The pull is winning and belonging, more than the purchase itself.
  • The community. People answer each other's questions on a stranger's behalf. "Give Jeremy his hearts." The switch from passive watcher to member is about identity.
Regular commerce serves demand you already have. Live creates it in the room, something Amazon and Walmart are not built to do.

2 Why it matters, the market

When commerce goes live, it performs. Industry figures eBay itself presents:

10xengagement vs standard video
~10xconversion vs standard e-commerce

eBay does ~$80B GMV a year across 134M buyers who came to shop, with trusted, authenticated goods and thirty years of data behind them. Live is already growing ~7x year-over-year while only a limited set of sellers can host. The opportunity is simple: make it easy enough to go live that supply catches up to the demand that is already there.

3 The players and the rules

Every live platform faces the same two risks, fraud and brand-safety on one side, seller skill on the other, and makes a different bet. Each is built on a different job.

What I saw as a user confirms the split. On Whatnot I built a full show in five minutes and watched a host sell Yeezy slides in three-second auctions to 7,000 people, a community manager in a seller's apron. On eBay the sellers who got in were reactive and in their heads, passing stickers, buyers asking for refunds in the chat, one host shouting "eBay glitched out, put in your max bids." Same job, one platform makes the seller great, the other makes them fight the tool.

4 Why eBay, the three truths

Intent alignment. On TikTok you came to be entertained, so a live selling diamonds reads as bait-and-switch. On eBay you came to shop, so community arrives as a bonus on intent you already had. Sticky, not manipulative.

The moat. It is not luxury, Whatnot has watches too. The moat is the data and trust under every seller: thirty years of catalog, sold-comps, history, and physical authentication. Exactly what an agent needs to stand up a trusted show.

The gap eBay is hired to close. eBay promises trusted community commerce and hands its sellers a manual, unsupported grind, to almost no one. That gap between the promise and the delivery is the entire strategy space.

5 Why is eBay stuck, as hypotheses

The question worth asking is why eBay chose the most restrictive model of the four platforms, and which explanation to test first. A few hold up.

  • Brand + velocity (strong). A fake damages a thirty-year reputation. Live moves faster than eBay's async fraud tools, so it polices the person up front, not the content live.
  • Unit economics (strongest). Streaming costs money per minute. Open access, where most sellers stream for hours and sell little, likely runs at negative unit economics. A static listing is near-pure margin.
  • Legacy tech (plausible). The live stack is bolted onto an old async system. Field evidence supports it, but it stays a hypothesis until eng confirms.
  • Demand-side (falsify this). Maybe the live audience is not there yet. This one would break the thesis, so it is the one to actively test.
The real framing is gated-and-profitable versus open-and-money-losing. The agent changes the unit economics of every stream, adding supply and making each stream pay, so eBay can open the gate without losing money.

6 The seller's real problem

They love the connection, they drown in the operations. Every seller I asked named community as their favorite thing, and every pain they named was ops: combined shipping, glitches, no-pays, "they say they never got it, tracking says delivered." The seller runs a creative workflow and an operations workflow in parallel, live.

The tax scales with volume, the magic per hour is capped by human attention. Success itself grows the tax until it caps the best sellers or burns them out.

7 Segmentation, who we build for first

Segment by the job, not the company size. The right first segment gives the most activation for the least cost and risk, and four tests decide it: the supply is already listed, the streams are profitable, only eBay can activate them, and no one is serving them yet.

  • Dormant high-value existing seller. Already sells on eBay, in categories where authentication matters, and has never gone live. Clears all four tests, which is why we start here.
  • Scaling host. Already live, loves it, hitting the ops ceiling. Real, but a tiny gated population.
  • Fast-mover, cheap goods. TikTok and Whatnot's game. Chasing it is the stuck-in-the-middle death.

eBay's lane is the intelligent platform for serious, curated selling. That is a lane Whatnot cannot occupy, and exactly where eBay's data and authentication pay off.

8 Differentiation, where we win

  • Below, trade away: out-communitying Whatnot, breadth of cheap goods, being the lowest-fee option.
  • Table stakes, match: go live from a phone, fast payout, discovery, AI listing (eBay already ships these).
  • Differentiate, win: the agent that builds trusted supply from existing data in one tap, trust carried into the live moment (in-stream comps, authenticity guarantee, video receipt), and the humans-do-human, agent-does-the-rest division of labor.

9 The strategy

Two picks compound into an activation flywheel. Pick one is the dormant high-value seller. Pick two is agentic one-tap setup, clearing the operations that keep them from going live. Together they turn the wheel: setup is cleared, the seller goes live and the show works, confidence and awareness build on their own, buyers gather, and more dormant sellers convert. The constraint was never demand, it was supply liquidity, and this is the single push that releases it.

Humans do the human part. The agent does the rest. Never touch the chat, the banter, shouting out loyal followers, reading the room. Automate everything else: the show setup, pricing, shipping, disputes, logistics.

10 The product, the one-tap host

Beat one, cold start. A dormant seller with 25 authenticated sneakers taps once. The agent reads the catalog, builds and prices the show from comps, stamps the authenticity guarantee and prepares video-receipt proof, and the seller reviews in seconds, then just talks.

Beat two, in-show. While the seller hosts, the agent runs the ops silently on a rail the buyer never sees: combined shipping, no-pays, buyer offers, "you lost that bid, here's the same item shipping Tuesday." The humans keep doing what community does.

Whatnot makes a human do all of this by hand. eBay's agent does it from data Whatnot does not have.

11 Challenges, and where we invest

The main investment is the real-time context layer: a live, structured view of the seller's catalog, comps, authentication status, buyer history, and show state, fast enough to act inside a stream. Think a knowledge graph over eBay's data plus an event layer for the live moment. The model is the easy part.

  • The cold-start build can be slower and richer, it runs before the show.
  • The in-show agent must be low-latency, leaning on the pre-built context layer.
  • Anything irreversible (an offer, a price drop, a refund) keeps a human gate. The agent proposes, the human confirms.

Reliability of the stream is the floor. Get the real-time layer solid first, and it is the place I would want leadership's read early.

13 Roadmap

Near-term, the bet we prototype. One-tap conversion of a static catalog into a live, priced, trust-stamped show, aimed at the dormant high-value seller. The verified authenticity claim gives it credibility and hype from day one.

Long-term, the vision. Once supply is flowing, the agent turns to the communal layer: offers, deals, giveaways on the seller's behalf, surfacing loyal buyers. Then the bigger swing: if any seller can go live in one tap, anyone can sell anything live. eBay was always the global treasure hunt. This makes the treasure hunt live.

14 Risks and open questions

  • The demand hypothesis. If eBay's data shows the live audience is the real constraint, the priority shifts to the buyer side. I would want that data first.
  • Quality dilution. Opening supply could fill the feed with weak shows. The agent raises the floor of every show, and we scale inside trusted categories first.
  • Reaching non-live sellers. Seller Hub, the app, and existing seller-incentive programs.
  • Cannibalization. Early hypothesis: live grows the pie, because it converts want-based demand async never captures.
